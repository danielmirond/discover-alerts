Kombucha has gone from a health-food-store curiosity to occupying entire supermarket aisles. The global market has surged over the past few years, with no sign of slowing down. But not all kombucha is created equal: the difference between a live-fermented artisanal brew and a pasteurized industrial product is the difference between natural yogurt and a sugar-laden dairy dessert.

<EvidenceBadge level={2} label="Moderate evidence" />

## What kombucha is

Kombucha is tea fermented by a SCOBY (Symbiotic Culture of Bacteria and Yeast), a symbiotic colony of acetic acid bacteria (*Gluconacetobacter*, *Acetobacter*) and yeasts (*Saccharomyces*, *Zygosaccharomyces*). Fermentation lasts 7-14 days and produces:

- **Organic acids:** acetic, gluconic, lactic
- **Live probiotics:** beneficial bacteria and yeasts
- **Enzymes:** proteases, lipases
- **B vitamins:** especially B1, B6, B12
- **Tea polyphenols:** catechins, theanine (from the tea base)

### What the evidence says

The evidence for kombucha is promising but limited:

- **In vitro:** antimicrobial, antioxidant, and anti-inflammatory activity demonstrated in multiple studies
- **Animal models:** improvements in metabolic markers, liver protection, glucose reduction
- **Humans:** very few controlled clinical trials. A 2024 RCT (Kapp & Sumner) in prediabetics showed a reduction in fasting glucose with 240ml/day over 4 weeks

The honest take: most kombucha claims extrapolate from in vitro or animal data. The probiotic benefits depend on the kombucha containing live microorganisms (not pasteurized).

## The key distinction: pasteurized vs unpasteurized

| Characteristic | Unpasteurized | Pasteurized |
|---|---|---|
| **Live probiotics** | Yes (millions of CFU) | No (killed by heat) |
| **Fermentation** | Continues in bottle | Stopped |
| **Shelf life** | 3-6 months (refrigerated) | 12+ months (ambient) |
| **Carbonation** | Natural (fermentation) | Artificially added |
| **Gut health benefit** | Probable | Organic acids only |

**Golden rule:** if the kombucha is sitting on a shelf at room temperature, it is pasteurized. If it is in the refrigerator, it probably is not. Read the label: "contains live cultures" is the signal.

## How to read the label

### Signs of quality

- **"Contains live cultures"** or "unpasteurized"
- **Residual sugar below 5g/100ml** (the initial sugar is consumed during fermentation)
- **Refrigerated** in store
- **Simple ingredients:** tea, water, sugar, SCOBY, fruit/spices
- **No artificial sweeteners**

### Red flags

- Shelf life exceeding 12 months (definitely pasteurized)
- Stored at room temperature
- "Kombucha flavor" or "kombucha aroma" (not real kombucha)
- Sugar above 8g/100ml (it is a soft drink with marketing)
- Ingredients such as sucralose, acesulfame K

## Consumption protocol

- **Dose:** 200-350ml per day
- **Timing:** with meals or between meals (acetic acid may aid digestion)
- **Start gradually:** 100ml/day for the first week if you are not accustomed to fermented foods
- **Avoid excess:** more than 500ml/day may cause digestive discomfort from the acidity
