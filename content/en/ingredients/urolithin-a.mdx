Urolithin A is one of the most compelling compounds to emerge from longevity research in the past decade. It is not a plant extract or a vitamin — it is a postbiotic metabolite that your gut microbiome produces (or fails to produce) from the ellagitannins found in pomegranate, walnuts, and certain berries. The problem is that only about 40% of the population has the gut flora needed to produce it.

<EvidenceBadge level={2} label="Moderate evidence" />

## What is urolithin A

Urolithin A (UA) is a metabolite produced by gut bacteria of the *Gordonibacter* and *Ellagibacter* genera from ellagitannins — polyphenols abundant in pomegranate, walnuts, raspberries, and blackberries. It does not exist as such in any food: it is the result of a microbial transformation in the colon.

What makes urolithin A special is its mechanism of action: it is the first natural compound identified that selectively activates **mitophagy** — the process by which cells eliminate damaged or dysfunctional mitochondria and replace them with new ones.

### The bioconversion problem

Not everyone produces urolithin A. Studies from Amazentis (the Swiss company behind Mitopure) and EPFL show that the ability to convert ellagitannins into urolithin A depends entirely on the composition of the microbiome:

- **~40%** of the population produces UA in meaningful quantities
- **~30%** produces minimal or intermediate amounts
- **~30%** does not produce UA at all

This explains why eating pomegranate does not guarantee the benefits of urolithin A — and why direct supplementation makes sense.

## Mechanism of action: mitophagy

Mitochondria are the energy powerhouses of the cell. With age, they accumulate damage and lose efficiency — a process directly linked to muscle aging, fatigue, and neurodegeneration.

Mitophagy is the quality control system: it identifies damaged mitochondria, tags them for destruction, and facilitates the generation of new ones. Urolithin A activates this process through:

1. **PINK1/Parkin activation** — the primary mitophagy signaling pathway
2. **BNIP3L induction** — a mitophagy receptor on the mitochondrial membrane
3. **Increased mitochondrial biogenesis** — it does not just eliminate damaged mitochondria but also stimulates the production of new ones
4. **Reduced oxidative stress** — by removing mitochondria that produce excess ROS

The net result: a younger, more efficient mitochondrial pool in muscle, cardiac, and neuronal cells.

## Clinical evidence

### EPFL/Amazentis 2019 study (Nature Metabolism)

The first clinical trial in humans (Andreux et al., 2019) administered 250mg, 500mg, and 1000mg of urolithin A to sedentary adults over 4 weeks. Results:

- Significant improvement in mitochondrial function biomarkers
- Reduction in plasma acylcarnitines (a marker of mitochondrial dysfunction)
- Improved mitophagy gene expression in muscle biopsies
- No relevant adverse effects at any dose

### ATLAS 2022 study (JAMA Network Open)

The most important study to date: a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. 66 older adults (65–90 years) received 1000mg of Mitopure for 4 months.

- **+12% in knee extension strength** vs placebo
- Improved muscular endurance (distance covered in 6 minutes)
- Reduction in inflammation biomarkers (CRP, IL-6)
- Excellent safety profile

### Ongoing studies

Amazentis has multiple active trials in sarcopenia, cognitive function, and joint health. The FDA granted GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) status to urolithin A in 2018.

## Natural sources of precursors

Although direct supplementation is more reliable, foods rich in ellagitannins (precursors) include:

| Food | Ellagitannins (mg/100g) | Conversion to UA |
|---|---|---|
| **Pomegranate (juice)** | 150–300 | Variable (40% of people) |
| **Walnuts** | 60–120 | Variable |
| **Raspberries** | 40–80 | Variable |
| **Strawberries** | 20–40 | Low |
| **Blackberries** | 30–60 | Variable |

The problem: even when consuming these foods daily, the amount of urolithin A produced is unpredictable and generally lower than the therapeutic dose of 500–1000mg.

## Dosage and protocol

**Standard dose:** 500–1000mg of urolithin A daily
**Timing:** with breakfast or the main meal (absorption improves with food)
**Minimum duration:** 8–12 weeks for measurable effects on muscle biomarkers

### 500mg or 1000mg?

- **500mg** — maintenance dose, sufficient to activate mitophagy
- **1000mg** — the dose used in the ATLAS study with adults over 65, with more pronounced results in muscle strength

## Safety and side effects

Urolithin A has an excellent safety profile:

- **GRAS** status granted by the FDA (2018)
- No serious adverse effects reported in clinical trials
- No significant known drug interactions
- Well tolerated in older adults (ATLAS study, 65–90 years)
- Rare side effects: mild gastrointestinal discomfort in the first few days

## Who should consider it

**Yes:** adults over 40 interested in mitochondrial and muscle health, especially those who do not regularly consume foods rich in ellagitannins. People with early-stage sarcopenia or muscle fatigue.

**Not yet justified:** young, active individuals with a polyphenol-rich diet. The cost-benefit ratio is not justified for those under 35 without specific conditions.
